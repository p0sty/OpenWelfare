This document outlines how customers may be able to claim compensation if they believe Services Australia has made a mistake that has caused them financial loss or other detriment. This information helps staff support customers who wish to make a compensation claim.

Reasons for compensation claims

Typically, compensation claims are made by customers who have suffered a loss:

  • because they relied on incorrect or incomplete advice provided by an agency officer
  • (such as a personal injury) as a consequence of a wrongful act of the agency or one of its officers
  • because an agency officer failed to do something where the agency had an obligation to do something
  • because of defective administrative processes within the agency, or
  • because of a breach of privacy by the agency or one of its officers

Before making a claim

Not all mistakes made by the agency can be remedied by a compensation payment. For example, compensation may not be payable if there is a legislative mechanism that can remedy the mistake (a legislative right of review).

If a customer wants to claim compensation or an act of grace payment, encourage and help them to:

  • consider whether there is a quicker and simpler way to address their concern
  • make sure they have exhausted any available review and appeal options, including a formal review and review by the Administrative Appeals Tribunal if appropriate). If the review and appeal process is unlikely to provide an outcome for the customer, it may be appropriate for them to lodge a claim under the Scheme for Compensation for Detriment caused by Defective Administration. Service Officers can contact the Customer Compensation Team for advice and information on 1800 995 496. Note that time limits may apply to lodging an appeal at the AAT
  • read the information on the Services Australia website about claiming compensation

Note: never discuss the possible outcome of a compensation claim.

The legal or administrative mechanism used to deal with a compensation claim will vary depending on the nature of the claim and whether the agency considers it may be legally liable if the matter went to court. See below for more information.

Legal and administrative mechanisms

Legal liability

If it is reasonable to conclude that the agency would be found liable if the matter were litigated in court, a claim must be settled in accordance with Appendix C: Handling Monetary Claims set out in the Legal Services Directions 2017 issued by the Attorney-General under section 55ZF of the Judiciary Act 1903.

Compensation for Detriment caused by Defective Administration (CDDA) Scheme

This is a discretionary mechanism which allows the agency to pay compensation when a person or organisation has suffered detriment as a result of the agencyâ€™s defective administration and when there is no legal requirement to make a payment.

Compensation claims are assessed by the Customer Compensation Team per guidelines issued by the Department of Finance.

Act of grace payments

The act of grace power is intended to ensure consistency and equity in the impact of government activities where other legislative and administrative provisions do not take sufficient account of the unique circumstances of individual cases.

Act of grace decisions are made by the Department of Finance. Section 65 of the Public Governance, Performance and Accountability Act 2013 allows the Finance Minister or their delegate to make payments that would not otherwise be authorised by law.
