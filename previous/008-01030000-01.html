This document outlines what to consider when helping customers with a permanent and diagnosed disability or medical condition.

Disabilities and medical conditions

Service Officers should be aware of the disabilities and medical conditions that may impact customers including:

  • musculo-skeletal conditions
  • mental health conditions
  • intellectual disabilities
  • Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV)/ acquired immune deficiency syndrome (AIDS)
  • acquired brain injury
  • vision loss
  • deaf, have hearing loss or, have a speech disability
  • childhood disabilities
  • common medical conditions for aged customers
  • developmental disorders

Understanding the challenges faced by customers with disability

Many customers with a disability or medical condition face barriers and challenges in life. Having an understanding of these barriers and challenges will ensure that Service Officers can help customers and manage issues that may arise.

When providing services for people with a disability or medical condition, focus on what people can do, rather than what they cannot do.

Customers with the same type of disability may have different functional abilities, limitations and methods of coping. Many customers with a disability or medical condition operate independently and require little or no support. Others, however, may require constant care and attention and may also have a nominee acting on their behalf.

Employment Services Assessment (ESAt) and Job Capacity Assessments (JCA)

Employment Services Assessments (ESAt) and Job Capacity Assessments (JCA) help in determining the extent of a customer’s work capacity, barriers to participation, referrals to appropriate services, and where applicable, assessment of medical conditions under the Social Security (Tables for the Assessment of Work-related Impairment for Disability Support Pension) Determination 2011 (the Impairment Tables).

The objective of the ESAt/JCA is to provide a comprehensive assessment of a customer's capacity to work. It examines barriers to participation and how these barriers are being addressed or need to be addressed. It assesses a customer's work capacity and recommends referrals to appropriate services.

Role of a Service Officer

Being aware of a customer's disability and being able to understand how medical conditions can affect them helps in providing appropriate assistance. Service Officers do not need to have an extensive medical knowledge but rather are expected to have a general understanding and awareness of disability issues. They need to be aware of suitable services and assistance that may be offered to the customer.

Participation needs to be viewed as a positive outcome for the customer. Encouraging participation and focusing on ability as well as disability assists in supporting people choosing to stay actively engaged and thereby reducing long-term dependence on income support.

Payments and support services

A Service Officer should be aware that there is a wide range of support services and payments for customers with disability or medical conditions and for their carers, including:

  • Australian Disability Enterprises
  • Disability Employment Services
  • Supported Wage System
  • Disability Support Pension
  • Mobility Allowance
  • JobSeeker Payment (JSP) and Youth Allowance (YA) (Incapacitated)
  • Carer Payment
  • Carer Allowance
  • Continence Aids Payment Scheme
  • Alternative letter format for vision impaired customers
  • Social work services
  • Community Engagement Officers
  • People with disability Services Australia website
  • Australian Government’s JobAccess website
