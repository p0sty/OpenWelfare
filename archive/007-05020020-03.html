1

Customer has claimed Family Tax Benefit (FTB), Parental Leave Pay (PPL) or Dad and Partner Pay (DAP) + Read more ...

Only use this procedure when assessing residence information for FTB, PPL and DAP claims.

For current FTB customers, it may be necessary to update:

  • country of residence
  • visa information for legal residence status
  • Australian citizenship details

To grant an FTB claim, the claimant must satisfy the residence requirements at the date of claim. The partner does not have to meet the residence requirements for FTB to be paid. However, under social security law, the partner must meet the residence requirements (and provide proof) for Health Care Card (HCC) purposes, which includes being noted as a partner on the customer's FTB maximum rate HCC.

If the claimant is subject to the Newly Arrived Residents Waiting Period (NARWP) for FTB Part A or is otherwise not qualified and has a partner, the partner’s circumstances should be investigated. If the partner is not subject to the NARWP for FTB Part A, for example the claimant’s partner may be an Australian citizen or the holder of a visa that exempts them from the NARWP for FTB Part A, attempts should be made to contact the claimant to advise them that FTB Part A may be payable if their partner claims FTB.

For a DAP claim to be granted, claimants must satisfy the residence requirements for each day in their DAP period (of up to 14 days).

To grant PPL the claimant must satisfy the residence requirements:

  • For children born or adopted on or after 1 July 2023:
    • Residence requirements remain unchanged
    • All claimants must meet the residence requirements on the date of birth or date of entry into care and on each of their PPL days in order to be eligible
    • For an initial eligibility determination, all claimants must meet the residence requirements on the date of determination. This is consistent with the current requirement for primary claimants
    • As the PPL period is being removed, there will be no requirement for claimants to meet the residence requirements between PPL days
  • For children born or adopted before 1 July 2023:
    • To be eligible for the PPL period, primary claimants must satisfy the residence requirements from the date of birth or adoption of the child until the end of their PPL period. Note: primary PPL claimants must complete the NARWP before their child's birth or entry to care to meet residency requirements. Payment cannot be transferred to a secondary claimant if a primary claimant is still serving their NARWP on or after their child's date of birth or entry to care
    • To be eligible for the PPL period, secondary or tertiary claimants must satisfy the residence requirements from the date they became the primary carer of the child until the end of their PPL period
    • Flexible PPL days can be paid up to 30 dates nominated by the customer. To be paid Flexible PPL the customer must meet residence requirements on their nominated Flexible PPL date

Father and partner payability is no longer dependent on the birth mother meeting the residence requirements.

If the PPL claimant ceases to meet the residence requirements at any point after the grant of their claim, for example their qualifying visa is cancelled, their entitlement to PPL ceases from last day residentially qualified plus 1 day. See Changing Australian residence details for family assistance and Paid Parental Leave scheme customer/child.

Stillborn Baby Payment (SBP) has the same residence requirements as FTB. To be eligible for SBP, a claimant must meet the FTB residence requirements within 26 weeks of the day of the child's delivery, or qualify for FTB for another child within 52 weeks of the stillbirth. However, they do not have to serve the NARWP.

3

Determine if the customer is living in Australia + Read more ...

Only customers who intend to reside in Australia are eligible to be granted FTB, PPL or DAP. A Service Officer must be satisfied the person is now residing in Australia. See Assessing if a customer is an Australian resident for assistance in determining the claimant's country of residence.

Ensure the Country of Residence (CRES) screen is coded correctly with claimant’s country of residence.

If FTB, PPL or DAP was granted as the customer indicated they would be living in Australia, but upon review it is found they have not made sufficient efforts to 'put down roots' in Australia as they had implied, their entitlement to payment from date of grant may be reviewed.

If there is subsequently sufficient evidence to support the fact the customer never intended to reside in Australia, there may be a debt raised owing to the Commonwealth from the date of grant.

Customers may claim and be eligible for family assistance and/or Paid Parental Leave scheme payments while temporarily overseas.

Is the customer residing in Australia?

  • Yes, and the CRES screen:
    • is coded to reflect this, go to Step 4
    • not coded to reflect this, correct the coding, then go to Step 4
  • No:
    • FTB customers, go to Step 11
    • PPL and DAP customers, go to Step 12

12

Customer is not residentially qualified for PPL or DAP + Read more ...

The customer does not satisfy residence requirements for PPL or DAP. The claim will reject.

Service Officers must check the assessment and only grant PPL when the claimant has been residentially qualified for every day from the date of birth. Where the assessment result is incorrect, investigate the details through normal escalation processes, for example, referral to a National ICT Referral Officer (NIRO) or Service Support Officer (SSO).

For details on coding and finalising the claim, go to Step 13.

Ensure residence information is recorded + Read more ...

The system will assess the customer's or children's residence qualification as long as the residence information is correctly recorded.

If the customer is a special category visa holder and not born in New Zealand, code the country of citizenship as New Zealand to ensure the correct residence qualification is assessed.

If the residence information is already recorded from a previous claim, no further information will be necessary. If residence information is incomplete, activate the Immigration Datalink to obtain the necessary information. For assistance, see Activating the Department of Home Affairs datalink and contingency procedures if datalink is unavailable.

Record residence information if the rejection is for any other reason. If residence details are not recorded, the system will override any other rejection and the claim will reject for a residence related reason.

For FTB only, if a child was born overseas to Australian residents, the country of residence will default to the child's country of birth and will reject the claim. For PPL and DAP, there are no residence requirements for children See, Country of Residence (CRES) screen for coding the child's County of Residence (CHCRES) screen.

Family member of refugee or former refugee + Read more ...

For the purpose of the NARWP and Qualifying Residence exemptions, family members include the following:

  • A partner of the person
  • A dependent child of the person
  • Another person who, in the opinion of the Secretary, should be treated for the purposes of this definition as a partner or dependent child of the person

  • No, go to Step 13

If the arrived relative is now an Australian citizen, they are no longer considered a refugee (they are a former refugee).

Customer must serve the NARWP + Read more ...

To serve the NARWP the customer must be physically present in Australia for the relevant period (52 weeks for FTB Part A and 104 weeks for PPL/DAP), as an Australian resident, special category visa holder or as the holder of a specified visa subclass.

FTB

If the FTB customer:

  • will be assessed as ineligible for FTB Part A and B and has a partner who may not be subject to the NARWP for FTB Part A, contact with the customer is not required as the claim will be rejected and a letter issued. The partner can lodge a claim for FTB. Continue to assess the claim
  • is not partnered, continue to assess the claim
  • will be assessed to receive FTB Part B and partner is not subject to the NARWP, go to Step 15

PPL/DAP

For children born or adopted on or after 1 July 2023, father and partner payability is no longer dependent on the birth mother having served, or being exempt from the NARWP.

To be eligible for the PPL period PPL claimants must complete the NARWP before their child's birth or entry to care to meet residence requirements.

To be eligible for a PPL day, the customer must satisfy the residence requirements on the date of birth or adoption of the child and they must also have completed the NARWP the day before their nominated PPL day.

For children born or adopted before 1 July 2023, payment cannot be transferred to a secondary claimant if a primary claimant is still serving their NARWP on or after their child's date of birth or entry into care.

For claimants who are Australian residents, the end date of the NARWP is calculated and held on:

  • the FTB NARWP Explanation (FTBNEX) screen in Customer First for FTB, or
  • the PPL/DAP Waiting Periods Calculations (P1PAP) for PPL or DAP, in Customer First, accessed via the PPL Claim Summary (P1CS) screen

Use this when advising the customer of future entitlement.

FTB/PPL/DAP

If the system calculates the NARWP has not yet been served, and the customer was an Australian resident before 1 September 1994, record past periods of residence on the Country of Residence (CRES) screen.

If the claim is incorrectly granting when the customer has not served or is not exempt from the NARWP, check the Family Member/Refugee Code on the Residence Savings (RSS) screen. If FMC, FME or PRE is coded, refer case to CIS for exemption coding to be ended.

If claim is PPL or DAP only, finalise the claim. See Processing standalone and combined claims for family assistance and/or Paid Parental Leave scheme payments.

Customer partner not subject to NARWP + Read more ...

If the customer’s partner is not subject to the NARWP, for example, the customer’s partner may be an Australian citizen or the holder of a visa that exempts them from the NARWP for FTB Part A, make a genuine attempt to contact the customer to discuss that FTB Part A may be payable if the partner claims FTB.

Prior to calling, Desktop Electronic Messaging Capability (DEMC) may be used to advise the customer they will be receiving a phone call. See Centrelink letters online and Electronic Messaging.

If the customer is not contactable, issue a Q164 - Request to contact letter. Manually place the claim on hold. Claim activities must be held for a minimum of 22 days to allow extra time for mail delivery and for customers to respond.

Record details in the Progress of claim DOC. Details must include:

  • what information must be asked of the customer when they make contact
  • details of what information the Service Officer receiving the call must document to enable the claim to progress, i.e., does the customer want to withdraw their claim and been advised the partner needs to lodge a claim for FTB

Has customer contact attempts been successful?

  • Yes,
    • FTB customer wants to withdraw their claim see Withdrawal of claim and DOC outcome of call, including customer has been advised that partner needs to lodge a claim for FTB
    • FTB customer wants to proceed with their claim, continue to assess the claim
  • No, the customer hasn’t responded to the contact attempts, the claim is to be processed and assessed based on the information provided in the claim
    • The claim should not be rejected for failing to reply to correspondence where request to contact (Q164) was solely due to possible entitlement for partner
