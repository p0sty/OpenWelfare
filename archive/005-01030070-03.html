Interview preparation checklist

Table 1

Item

Description

1

Check preferred language + Read more ...

  • Do not assume language spoken based on country of birth. Refugees often move through various countries
  • Ask 'what is your preferred language'
  • Sometimes it may be hard to get interpreters for new emerging refugee languages so ask 'what other languages do you speak'

2

Book extra time for interviews + Read more ...

Extra time will be needed for interviews with refugees. Services Australia received additional funding to service refugee customers to cover this. There is also an additional component for interpreters.

3

Consider gender issues + Read more ...

People from some cultures may feel more at ease dealing with someone of the same sex, particularly when being asked to reveal personal information.

4

Physical environment + Read more ...

Try to conduct interviews in spaces that do not resemble cells, or behind locked doors. Leave the door open if in an interview room.

5

What is necessary to know about Post Traumatic Stress Disorder + Read more ...

The most common condition that affects refugees is post-traumatic stress disorder. This develops in people who have experienced extreme emotional or physical stress. It is a normal reaction to an abnormal situation.

Symptoms can fluctuate over time and increase during periods of stress. Symptoms include insomnia, panic attacks, hallucinations, impaired memory and concentration.

This can affect participation in employment and education. People affected may have trouble remembering an appointment or even being able to put their case as to why they have not been able to meet commitments.

Service Officers should not take the behaviour associated with post-traumatic stress personally. Periods of anger and hostility, or disinterest and withdrawal are common amongst survivors of torture. Their feelings of anger are usually not directed at Service Officers, but are a result of their experiences. Often, acknowledgment of a survivor's anger at the situation they find themselves in may help.

Conducting the interview

Table 2

Item

Description

1

Service Officer to check their body language + Read more ...

Refugees may have suffered harassment, interrogation and violence at the hands of authorities or government agencies.

To reduce anxiety and to encourage a feeling of safety, consider the impact of one's body language, hand and facial gestures, and tone of voice.

Simple things like smiling, making eye contact and using a normal tone of voice will help.

Consider different techniques on an individual basis. For example, some cultures do not feel comfortable with eye contact so do not force it.

Shaking hands with someone from the opposite sex can sometimes be culturally inappropriate, so in this situation let the customer take the lead.

2

Explain why questions need to be asked + Read more ...

Due to past experiences, survivors of torture may harbour distrust or fear of authority.

By clearly explaining the reasons for gathering personal information communication will open up.

3

Use plain English + Read more ...

Explain the purpose of the interview in plain English. People who can speak basic English cannot always understand complex information covered during an interview. Using plain English will also make it easier for an interpreter to interpret in a way the customer can understand.

Avoid extremes, although do not simplify information so much that it loses its meaning.

4

Explain unfamiliar concepts + Read more ...

Some people will come from countries with no social security system. It is important to explain unfamiliar concepts in plain English such as:

  • confidentiality
  • payments
  • services
  • how assessments and decisions are made, and
  • specialist services (for example, social workers, occupational psychologists)

5

Be sensitive about how questions are asked about missing family members + Read more ...

Many customers will have missing partners. They will not be sure where they are or whether they are alive.

Do not keep repeating the question expecting to get a different answer.

They may not have accepted that the person has died or genuinely do not know.

If an issue has already been answered earlier during another question, do not ask the question again just because it is on the form.

For some, talking about past extreme experiences unnecessarily can be detrimental to healing. Only issues that are directly relevant should be discussed at an interview.

6

Discuss strategies to address information overload on arrival + Read more ...

It helps to acknowledge to the customer that they may feel information overload on arrival, and it will help if strategies are provided to assist.

For example, advise the customer that although a lot of information will need to be covered, they do not need to worry if they cannot remember everything and can ask questions at any time. If necessary they can also call or come back to the office at a later date if they have any questions.

On the other hand, do not assume people know how the system works or know the questions to ask. Many refugees come from countries with no social security system.

7

Test understanding with open questions + Read more ...

To test understanding, use open ended questions that do not involve yes/no answers. Open questions also give the customer the opportunity to bring up pertinent information that may have been missed.

In some cultures 'yes' can mean 'no'. Nodding or saying 'yes' can mean 'I'm acknowledging your authority but I don't understand what youâ€™re saying'. Also a series of closed questions can sometimes appear like interrogation to victims of torture. Varying the question style can help here.

8

Service Officer to check their assumptions made about responses to standard question + Read more ...

Making assumptions based on past experience can be useful. However, it is important for the Service Officer to check their assumptions made about responses to standard Services Australia questions for refugees. Ask for more detail if not sure.

For example, in some cultures families are not just blood relations. If a child is taken into care by a neighbour in a refugee crisis, the neighbour may consider this child her own.

9

Use repetition + Read more ...

Customers who have problems with memory or concentration associated with post-traumatic stress may require patience in explaining details, or will need things to be explained more than once.

It is necessary to reinforce messages at future contacts. Often things will not mean anything until a customer has to do something about it.

Additional customer support

Table 3

Item

Description

1

Highlight obligations + Read more ...

Due to language barriers, customers may not respond to letters or complete forms.

Some refugees may end up with debts, or payments may be stopped, if they are not familiar with their obligations.

Remind customers about the importance of responding to letters and to contact Services Australia if there is anything they do not understand or if their situation changes.

Give practical examples that will put it in context, including what can happen if they do not respond.

2

Highlight rights + Read more ...

Because of past experiences with government, refugees may be hesitant to question authority. It is important to highlight their rights.

People suffering from post-traumatic stress disorder may find it particularly difficult to put their case forward as to why they have not been able to meet obligations. Again, open questions can be very useful in these situations so assistance can be better targeted.

3

Give customers CDs, DVDs and factsheets in their language + Read more ...

While face-to-face contact works best with refugee customers, there are also products that can be given to the customer to reinforce messages. These will allow the customer to revisit information as issues arise for them.

As many new and emerging communities have low literacy in their language, audio products are often the best option. Order the main languages for the customer demographics so there is a supply on hand.

Print on demand factsheets is available in many languages from the Services Australia website.

See the Resources page for a link to these pages.

4

Promote Multilingual Phone Service (MPS) + Read more ...

Some new communities arriving in Australia are not familiar with phones, so it may be useful to offer the Multilingual Phone Service (MPS).

Customers can call the MPS directly on 13 1202 to receive information and assistance about Centrelink payments and services in their preferred language.

This is particularly important for languages where connections to a third party interpreter may take a little longer.

5

Promote the free interpreter service for all contacts + Read more ...

Initially it is preferable to do as much contact as possible face-to-face, as it is much harder to pick up on misunderstandings over the phone. Remind the customer that the interpreter service is free. Always use a telephone or onsite interpreter if a customer has difficulty with English.

6

Consult the Multicultural Service Officer if help is needed + Read more ...

If there are any concerns about a particular case, including interactions with other service providers, call the local Multicultural Service Officer. See the Resources page for a link to this page.
